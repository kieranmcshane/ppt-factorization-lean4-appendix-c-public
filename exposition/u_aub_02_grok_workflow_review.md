# Grok Workflow Review for U-AUB-02

This note records the useful adversarial feedback from the bounded Grok review
run after U-AUB-358.  Grok is advisory only; Lean and the tracked ledger remain
the source of truth.

## Main Criticism

The workflow says clearly that U-AUB-02 is not done, but the long checked
endpoint name can still make an adapter feel like a near-final theorem.  The
reviewer called this a structural risk: humans may anchor on the checked
endpoint name instead of on the four open leaf groups.  This risk remains even
after child primitive absorption, because the new endpoint has a cleaner
surface but is still a conditional reduction.

## Article and Workflow Implication

The four theorem-strength leaves should be stated before, or at least
alongside, the checked endpoint:

1. large-gap left Biane adjacent link;
2. large-gap right Biane adjacent link;
3. side-specific residual return-interval collision, including the remaining
   nonwrapping smaller child internal-subinterval branch;
4. Chapuy/trisection recurrence.

The article and workflow documents should not let finite gap-three closures or
endpoint names rhetorically stand in for progress on those four leaves.

## Suggested Next Leaf

The reviewer judged the side-specific residual return-interval collision
group as the most plausible next proof target.  The reason is not that it is
easy; it is that the checked infrastructure now exists exactly for that leaf:
branch-normalized return intervals, side tags, primitive/internal-subinterval
splits, nonwrapping off-cycle refinements, block confinement, smaller
side-aware residual descent, adjacent-or-shorter raw-`T` splitting, and child
primitive absorption.  This is a structural recommendation, not proof evidence.

## Guardrail

Finite permutation enumeration remains conjecture-finding only.  It should be
kept separate from theorem-strength claims, especially because large-gap and
non-direct configurations may exhibit phenomena invisible in small checked
slices.  The reviewer singled out wrapping direction, non-fixed predecessor
skips, and smaller-child internal subintervals as the places where finite
enumeration is most likely to mislead if treated as more than conjecture
evidence.
